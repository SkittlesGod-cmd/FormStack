The Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 gave supplement brands permission to make claims — but it came with conditions most founders don't fully understand until their label is already at the printer.

The core rule is simple in theory: you can describe how a supplement affects the structure or function of the body, but you cannot claim it treats, cures, mitigates, or prevents a disease. In practice, the line between those two things is where most compliance problems live.

## The Two-Second Version

**Allowed:** "Supports healthy blood pressure already within normal range."
**Not allowed:** "Lowers high blood pressure."

**Allowed:** "Promotes relaxation and helps manage occasional stress."
**Not allowed:** "Treats anxiety disorders."

**Allowed:** "Supports joint comfort and mobility."
**Not allowed:** "Reduces arthritis symptoms."

The disease name is usually what triggers the violation. But it's not always that obvious.

## What Makes a Claim a Disease Claim

The FDA considers a claim to be a disease claim if it — explicitly or implicitly — suggests that the product treats or prevents a disease condition. That includes:

**Naming the disease directly.** "Helps with diabetes," "supports recovery from depression," "reduces symptoms of ADHD." Even if you hedge with "may help" or "supports," naming the disease is usually enough.

**Using a medical term associated with a disease.** "Lowers LDL cholesterol" edges into disease territory because elevated LDL is a medical marker for cardiovascular disease risk. "Helps maintain healthy cholesterol levels already in the normal range" is the compliant version.

**Referencing a disease's symptoms without naming it.** "Helps with the crushing chest pain that comes after eating" doesn't name GERD, but it's describing GERD symptoms. The FDA can still flag it.

**Citing disease-specific clinical evidence.** If your label says "clinically studied to reduce inflammation" and the study you're referencing used people with rheumatoid arthritis, you've imported a disease context even if your label copy doesn't name the disease.

## What You Can Say

Structure/function claims describe what the supplement does for a normal, healthy body. The clearest examples:

- "Supports immune function"
- "Promotes bone health"
- "Helps maintain normal blood sugar levels already in the normal range"
- "Supports cognitive function and mental clarity"
- "Helps maintain healthy gut flora"

The phrase "already in the normal range" is a useful compliance tool. It signals that you're talking about supporting normal physiology, not correcting a diseased state. It's not always required, but it shifts the framing.

## The Required Disclaimer

Every product making a structure/function claim must include the standard disclaimer:

> *"This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."*

This must appear on the label with "prominent placement" — same panel as the claim, in legible type. Many brands bury it in the fine print on the back panel. That's technically allowed if it's readable, but it doesn't give you cover if the rest of your label reads like a pharmaceutical.

The disclaimer is not a magic shield. It does not make an otherwise illegal disease claim legal. FDA enforcement has consistently held that a prominent disease claim followed by a fine-print disclaimer is still a disease claim.

## The 30-Day Notification Requirement

If you make a structure/function claim, you're required to notify the FDA within 30 days of first marketing. The notification goes to the Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition (CFSAN) and includes:

- Your name and address
- The text of the claim
- The name of the dietary ingredient or supplement

This is not a pre-approval process. FDA doesn't respond, approve, or reject your claim — you're just notifying them. But failing to notify is itself a regulatory violation, and it's an easy one to overlook when you're focused on the product launch.

## High-Risk Ingredients and Claims

Some ingredient-claim combinations attract more FDA attention than others. These are areas where the line between structure/function and disease claims is especially contested:

**Blood glucose:** "Supports healthy blood sugar" is generally fine. "Helps insulin sensitivity" is riskier because it references a mechanism tied to diabetes treatment. "Reduces blood sugar spikes" after a meal starts to read like a claim about a metabolic disease state.

**Cardiovascular:** "Supports heart health" is broad enough to be safe. "Helps maintain healthy cholesterol already in the normal range" is fine. "Reduces cardiovascular risk" is not.

**Mood and mental health:** "Promotes relaxation" and "helps manage occasional stress" are common and defensible. "Supports serotonin levels" is mechanistically interesting but invites comparison to antidepressant drugs. "Reduces depressive symptoms" is clearly a disease claim.

**Weight management:** "Helps maintain a healthy weight as part of a calorie-controlled diet" is generally acceptable. "Burns fat" or "reduces obesity" is not.

## Writing Compliant Copy That Still Converts

The fear with compliance is that scrubbing disease references will strip the claim of any persuasive power. That's not true — it requires more care, but compliant copy can be compelling.

The trick is anchoring the claim to the user's outcome, not the medical condition:

Instead of: "Helps with anxiety"
Try: "Promotes calm focus during high-pressure situations"

Instead of: "Reduces joint pain"
Try: "Supports comfortable, free-range movement"

Instead of: "Lowers blood pressure"
Try: "Supports cardiovascular health and healthy circulation"

You're saying the same thing — the ingredient is being used by people for these reasons — but you're describing the structure/function benefit rather than the disease treatment.

## Before Your Label Goes to Print

Run every claim through two questions:

1. Does this name a disease or disease symptom, directly or by implication?
2. Does a reasonable consumer reading this believe the product treats or prevents a disease?

If either answer is yes, rewrite before it costs you a warning letter.

FDA enforcement has increased in the supplement space, and structure/function violations are a common trigger. The upside is that the rules are clear enough that a careful review of your label copy before launch catches the vast majority of issues.
