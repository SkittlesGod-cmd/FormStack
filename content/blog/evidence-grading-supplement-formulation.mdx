When you're building a supplement formulation, the single most important question isn't "does this ingredient work?" It's "how confident should I be that it works at this dose, in this population, for this outcome?"

That distinction drives the difference between a product that delivers on its promise and one that gets torn apart in a review thread or, worse, draws regulatory scrutiny.

## What Evidence Grades Actually Mean

At FormLayer, every ingredient gets an evidence grade — A, B, or C — based on the volume and quality of human clinical evidence supporting its use for your stated health goal.

**Grade A** — Supported by multiple large, well-designed randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in humans. Effect sizes are consistent across trials, and the dose range is well-established. You can make confident claims and expect predictable outcomes.

**Grade B** — Supported by some human RCTs, but the evidence base has limitations: small sample sizes, short durations, inconsistent effects across trials, or limited replication. The ingredient is plausibly effective, but at meaningful confidence intervals.

**Grade C** — Limited human clinical data. Evidence may come from animal models, in vitro studies, epidemiological data, or very small pilot studies. Often promising mechanistically, but not yet validated at scale in humans.

## Why Grade C Isn't Automatically "Don't Use"

Grade C ingredients get misunderstood. A C rating doesn't mean an ingredient is ineffective — it means the human evidence is still developing.

Magnesium threate was Grade C three years ago. So was apigenin. Both had compelling mechanistic rationales and early-stage human data, and both are now increasingly common in sleep and cognitive formulations as the evidence base grows.

The question is whether a Grade C ingredient fits your product's positioning. A "science-backed" formulation should lead with Grade A and B ingredients for its primary claims. Grade C ingredients work well in a support role where you're not hanging a marketing claim on them.

## The Dose Problem

Evidence grade only tells part of the story. A Grade A ingredient can still be underdosed — and an underdosed formulation is, functionally, a different product than the one studied.

Ashwagandha (KSM-66) is Grade A for stress and cortisol reduction. The effective dose from the RCTs that established that evidence is **300–600 mg/day of the extract**. Drop it to 50 mg to save cost and you've created a product that looks like it has ashwagandha, but doesn't have ashwagandha at a studied dose. That's not a formulation decision — it's a label decision.

FormLayer flags dose ranges from published human RCTs specifically because the dose is where the evidence lives. The clinical trial studied 600 mg, not "some amount."

## Communicating Evidence to Manufacturers and Partners

Manufacturers don't need a research brief. They need to know what you're making and why. But co-manufacturers, investors, and retail buyers do ask about evidence — and having grades gives you a language for that conversation.

"This formulation uses Grade A and B ingredients for all primary claims" is a defensible, credible statement. It signals that you've done the work and aren't just pattern-matching off a trending ingredient list.

The same framing helps when you're iterating. If your cost-reduction review is looking at substitutions, you can filter the decision through evidence grade: "we can sub in compound X at a lower cost, but it drops from Grade A to Grade B — is that acceptable for this SKU?"

## What to Do With Mixed-Grade Formulations

Most real formulations have a mix of grades. A typical cognitive or athletic performance formula might look like:

- 3–4 Grade A ingredients covering the primary mechanism
- 2–3 Grade B ingredients with strong mechanistic rationale and partial human data
- 1–2 Grade C ingredients added for their emerging potential

That's not a problem — that's honest formulation. The error is treating every ingredient the same, making identical claims regardless of evidence confidence, and not communicating the distinction internally or externally.

Review your evidence grades when writing label copy. Grade A and B ingredients can support structure/function claims with more confidence. Grade C ingredients should be described in terms of their mechanism or role in the formula, not in terms of clinical outcomes you don't have solid RCT support for.

## The Bottom Line

Evidence grading is a tool, not a verdict. It helps you allocate confidence, make defensible substitution decisions, write tighter claims, and communicate credibly with everyone who touches the product. Used correctly, it makes your formulations better and your conversations with manufacturers, buyers, and regulators cleaner.

The worst outcome in supplement formulation isn't picking a Grade C ingredient. It's not knowing which of your ingredients are Grade A and which are Grade C — and treating them identically because you never checked.
